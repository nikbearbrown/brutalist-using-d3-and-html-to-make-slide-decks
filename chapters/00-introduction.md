# Introduction

It is 11:42 p.m. on a Thursday. You have lecture at 9 a.m. The deck is forty-three slides long, generated by an AI tool you trust enough to use but not enough to ship without checking. Slide twelve is a paragraph from chapter 6 of the textbook with one word bolded. Slide nineteen is a five-color bar chart of three categorical variables. Slide twenty-seven has a stock photo of a stethoscope on it, for reasons you cannot reconstruct. The slides look professional. They look done. They look wrong.

You try to fix slide twelve. You drag the text box left. The bolded word loses emphasis because the background isn't dark enough now. You change the background. The headline contrast drops below readable. You undo. You try again. You give up on slide twelve, move to slide nineteen, and within ninety seconds you have produced a different version of the same problem.

It is not 11:42 anymore. It is 12:38. The deck is no better.

This book is about the gap between an AI slide tool that can generate a deck and a faculty member who can improve one. The tools are competent. The faculty member is competent. What is missing is a working vocabulary for what is wrong — a set of names for the failure modes that, once you can see them, become fixable in one move instead of forty.

The central argument is this: **you can't prompt precisely if you can't see precisely.** AI slide tools — Canva, Gamma, Beautiful.ai, Tome, the Brutalist system, every system that follows them — produce output the user cannot improve because the user lacks the language to say what to change. The fix is not better tools. The fix is a trained eye. This book builds the eye. The slides are the medium. Brutalist is the cheap-iteration loop. The diagnostic vocabulary is the point.

This is a book for faculty who teach with slides. Not for designers. Not for instructional-design specialists. Not for the eight people in your department who already read Tufte. It is for the historian, the chemist, the political scientist, the engineer, the literature professor — anyone who has slides to make for tomorrow and no design training. You know your field. You do not yet have a name for what is wrong with the deck. That is what is being taught here.

## What this book is

A practitioner's handbook. Read it cover to cover once — twelve short chapters, each diagnostic — to build the vocabulary. Then use it as a reference. When something feels off about a deck, you will know which chapter to open.

The book teaches **two diagnostic questions per chapter** — one from cognitive science (Sweller, Mayer, the multimedia-learning literature) and one from visual design (Tufte, Reynolds, Bringhurst, the typography and information-design tradition). Always both. Always in that order: cognitive science first, because the slide's job is to support learning before it supports aesthetics; visual design second, because the slide's appearance is the apparatus through which the cognitive job happens.

Every chapter ends with a **before/after pair**, a **paste-able prompt** for the Brutalist system, and a **DESIGN.md change** — the variable in your persistent design configuration to update so that the failure stops happening to other slides. The vocabulary becomes a system.

## What this book is not

It is not a course textbook. The chapters are not week-sized. There are no exams. There is no comprehensive theory of design.

It is not a popular-science book about cognitive load. Sweller and Mayer are infrastructure, cited rarely and used constantly. If you want the academic version, *Multimedia Learning* (3rd edition, 2020) is in the back matter.

It is not the Brutalist system manual. Brutalist is the medium that makes the fixes cheap. The system documentation lives at [brutalist.art](https://www.brutalist.art/). This book is about what to tell the system, not how the system works internally.

It is not a complete treatment of accessibility law. Chapter 5 covers contrast ratios because contrast is a slide-design question. Full accessibility compliance is outside scope and is better served by your institution's accessibility office.

Prerequisites: comfort with the Brutalist system at a basic level — that is, you can edit a DESIGN.md and paste a prompt — or the willingness to learn. No prior D3 expertise. No prior design training. No prior cognitive-science background.

## A central concept that runs throughout

Watch for one phrase across the chapters: **the diagnostic question**. Every chapter teaches a pair of them. By chapter 12 you will have twenty-four diagnostic questions, and the questions, not the chapters, are what you will carry. The book's final chapter is the checklist — the questions collected on one page, do-confirm style, run once per deck before you ship.

When the book lands, you will not be reciting Mayer's Coherence Principle or Reynolds's slideument warning. You will be looking at a slide and asking, *can a student read this and listen to me at the same time?* The framework vocabulary is scaffolding. The diagnostic questions are the building.

## How this book is organized

Twelve chapters in two acts. Act One (chapters 1–5) trains the eye on individual slide problems. Act Two (chapters 6–11) moves from slide-level diagnosis to deck-level structure. Chapter 12 is the diagnostic checklist — the reference artifact you keep after the rest of the book has done its work.

**Act One — building the eye:**

- **Chapter 1, The Slideument Problem.** A deck that is exhausting to lecture from and useless to study from. Reynolds's term. The deck does not know what it is for.
- **Chapter 2, No Clear Hierarchy.** A slide where every element competes for attention. Mayer's Signaling Principle and Bringhurst's typographic hierarchy.
- **Chapter 3, Too Much Text.** A textbook paragraph pasted onto a slide. Mayer's Coherence and Modality Principles.
- **Chapter 4, The Wrong Visual Form.** Bullets where a 2×2 matrix would communicate instantly. Cleveland and McGill on perceptual ranking.
- **Chapter 5, Color Is Doing Nothing (or Harm).** Decoration where there should be encoding. WCAG contrast and ColorBrewer.

**Act Two — from slide to deck:**

- **Chapter 6, The Textbook Figure on the Slide.** A figure that worked in a textbook fails on a slide. Different reading conditions.
- **Chapter 7, Seductive Details.** The stock photo, the fun fact, the quirky cartoon — engagement that reduces learning. Harp and Mayer 1998.
- **Chapter 8, The Deck That Covers but Doesn't Teach.** A sixty-slide deck that touches every topic and builds no capability. Wiggins and McTighe on backward design.
- **Chapter 9, Live Deck vs. Study Artifact.** One deck cannot be both. The fix is producing two artifacts from one source.
- **Chapter 10, The Headline That Says Nothing.** "Introduction." "Methods." "Results." Topic labels are not claims. Atkinson on assertion-evidence.
- **Chapter 11, Owning Your DESIGN.md.** The meta-chapter. The vocabulary becomes your design system. The Brutalist defaults are starting points, not verdicts.
- **Chapter 12, The Diagnostic Checklist.** All twenty-four questions on one page. Do-confirm. Run it before every deck ships.

## How to read this book

Cover to cover, in order, the first time. The chapters compound. Chapter 2's hierarchy concept reappears in chapter 5 as color signal; chapter 1's slideument problem reappears in chapter 9 as the deck-level version of the same failure. Reading out of order works for reference later, not for building the eye now.

Each chapter is short — twenty to thirty minutes of reading. Each has the same nine-section anatomy: *the feeling · the two diagnostic questions · the principle named · bad example · good example · the prompt · the DESIGN.md change · the diagnostic questions to keep · what two people might disagree about*. Sections 4–7 (the before/after pair, the prompt, the DESIGN.md change) are the load-bearing ones. If you are skimming, those are the four you read.

Every chapter ends with diagnostic questions to keep. Those questions are also collected in chapter 12. The chapter you carry is chapter 12. The earlier chapters teach you what the questions mean.

## A note about AI

This book is about using AI to make slides. It is also about what AI cannot supply.

The Brutalist system generates slides from prompts. Canva, Gamma, Beautiful.ai do the same with different interfaces. All of them will produce a deck. None of them, today, will tell you whether the deck teaches what you want it to teach. The diagnostic judgment is yours. That is not because the models are insufficient. It is because the question "does this deck serve THIS student in THIS course at THIS moment in their learning?" is a question whose ground truth lives in your head, not in any model's training data. The model can ask plausible-sounding diagnostic questions. It cannot know which question matters here.

Throughout this book, the AI relationship is treated as a delegation problem with a hard boundary. You — the faculty member, the expert in your field — make the diagnostic call: *the headline is a label, not a claim*. You translate that judgment into a precise instruction: *rewrite the headline as the slide's claim, ≤12 words*. The AI tool executes. The result is fast, cheap, and revisable. The boundary holds because you are speaking the diagnostic vocabulary the model does not have.

This matters because the alternative — accepting AI slide output uncritically — is how most AI-generated decks end up looking polished and being wrong. The polish is the AI's job. The diagnosis is yours. A faculty member who can name what is wrong with a slide and say what would fix it is a faculty member who can use AI tools well. A faculty member who cannot name what is wrong is at the mercy of the model's defaults, and the model's defaults were optimized for slides that *look fine*, not for slides that *teach what they need to teach*.

The Brutalist system is one instantiation of this division of labor. The system holds the boundary explicitly: the design conversation belongs to the human; the rendering belongs to the model. Other systems can hold the same boundary, less explicitly. The point is not the system. The point is the boundary. Once you have the diagnostic vocabulary, you can hold it in any tool. Once you cannot, no tool will save you.

This is the larger argument the book sits inside. AI does not eliminate the need for design judgment. It moves design judgment upstream — out of the doing and into the asking. The doing gets cheap. The asking becomes everything. This book teaches the asking.

## Closing return

It is 11:42 p.m. on a Thursday. You have lecture at 9 a.m. The deck is forty-three slides. Slide twelve has too much text. You know now, in twenty seconds, what is wrong with it: the verbal channel is doing two jobs at once — Mayer's Redundancy Principle, plain language, slideument at the slide level. You open the Brutalist prompt window. You paste in eleven words: *Reduce body text to a six-word headline asserting the slide's claim; move full explanation to notes field*. The system runs. Slide twelve comes back legible. You move to slide nineteen with twenty-three minutes that you used to spend on slide twelve still ahead of you.

That is the book working.

Let's go.
