# Chapter 8 — The Deck That Covers but Doesn't Teach

> What should the student be able to *do* after this lecture — and does the deck build them to that, or just list what you intended to mention?

---

## 1. The feeling

You are preparing the lecture on capital structure. The textbook chapter is 90 pages. You open Gamma, paste in the chapter outline, and forty seconds later you have a 60-slide deck. It is comprehensive. Modigliani-Miller no-tax. Modigliani-Miller with-tax. Financial distress. Pecking order theory. Signaling. Agency costs. Each topic gets ten clean slides. The deck does not miss anything. You feel prepared.

You lecture from it. Fifty minutes, sixty slides, roughly one minute per slide, on the dot. The class is attentive. You finish exactly at the bell. You take three questions, all of which you can answer.

Two weeks later you give the unit test. The first question is the one you most want them to get right: *A mature firm with stable cash flows is considering raising $500M. Walk through whether they should issue debt or equity, and defend your choice using the trade-off theory of capital structure.* About a third of the class produces a coherent argument. About a third recognize the relevant concepts but cannot string them together. About a third write back definitions — what Modigliani-Miller is, what agency costs are, what the pecking order is — and stop. They could recognize every term you put in front of them. None of them could do the thing.

The deck covered the chapter. The deck did not teach the chapter. Those are different operations and they don't look different from the front of the room.

This is the feeling Chapter 8 is for. Not a failure of effort, of expertise, or of caring about the students. A failure of the *unit of analysis*. The deck was organized around the textbook's structure rather than around the build the students needed.

## 2. The two diagnostic questions

Ask both. In this order. Every deck. Every time. Before you make slide one.

**From cognitive science:** *What should the student be able to **do** after this lecture?* Not "know about." Not "have heard of." Not "be familiar with." *Do.* Action verbs from Bloom: explain, apply, analyze, compare, evaluate, produce. If the answer is "be familiar with capital structure theories," the deck is going to be a list. If the answer is "decide whether a firm with stable cash flows should take on debt, and defend the choice with the trade-off theory," the deck has somewhere to go.

This is the [backward design](https://www.ascd.org/books/understanding-by-design-expanded-2nd-edition?variant=103055) question, from Wiggins and McTighe's *Understanding by Design* ([2nd ed., 2005, ASCD](https://andymatuschak.org/files/papers/Wiggins,%20McTighe%20-%202005%20-%20Understanding%20by%20design.pdf)). Wiggins and McTighe call the failure to ask this question "the twin sin of unfocused activity and content coverage" — instruction that has either lots of doing without understanding, or lots of telling without doing. The corrective is structural: start from the desired outcome, work backward to the evidence that would show the learner has it, and only then design the instruction that builds toward it. The slide deck is the last step. Not the first.

**From visual design:** *Does the deck's structure reveal the conceptual arc, or just enumerate the topics?* Open a deck to its table of contents (most decks don't have one — that itself is a tell). If the section headings read like a textbook table of contents (*Section 1: Modigliani-Miller. Section 2: Financial Distress. Section 3: Pecking Order…*), the deck is organized for coverage. If the section headings name the moves of a build (*The puzzle. The first cut. What's still wrong. The synthesis.*), the deck is organized for teaching.

The two questions converge. The first names the outcome the lecture is supposed to produce. The second checks whether the deck's structure is in service of that outcome or in service of the textbook's organization. Most decks fail the second question because their authors never asked the first.

## 3. The principle named

Plain language first: **covering material is not the same as teaching it.** A deck that walks the student through every term in the chapter has confused *the territory* with *the route through the territory*. The territory is comprehensive. The route is selective, ordered, and bent toward the place the student is supposed to arrive.

In framework vocabulary, this is backward design. Three moves, in order:

1. **Identify the desired result.** What should the student be able to do at the end of the lecture? State it as an action.
2. **Determine acceptable evidence.** What would the student produce that would show they can do it? An answer to a problem, a defended decision, an explanation a peer can follow.
3. **Plan the learning experience.** What slides — and what *not*-slides — cause the student to be able to produce that evidence?

Wiggins and McTighe are explicit that step 3 is the last step. Most instructors invert the order: they start with the chapter ("what do I need to cover?"), generate slides around it, and only afterward consider what students should be able to do — if they consider it at all. Forward design starts from what you have. Backward design starts from where the student should land.

The empirical anchor for *why* backward design works is the cognitive science of how learning actually happens. Three findings, all settled in the literature:

**Retrieval practice produces learning; restudy mostly produces fluency.** [Karpicke and Roediger (2008)](https://www.science.org/doi/abs/10.1126/science.1152408), in *Science*, gave students three rounds of either studying a passage four times or studying it once and recalling it from memory three times. On a delayed test a week later, the recall group remembered roughly 60% of the material; the restudy group remembered roughly 40%. The students in the *restudy* condition felt more confident — they recognized the material — and learned less. The retrieval moment did the learning. A deck that ends when the content ends throws away the most powerful learning move available in the lecture.

**Distributed practice beats massed practice.** [Cepeda et al. (2006)](https://psycnet.apa.org/record/2006-04116-006), a meta-analysis in *Psychological Bulletin* covering 839 effects from 317 experiments, found a robust spacing effect: practice distributed across time produces better long-term retention than the same amount of practice concentrated in one session. For a lecture, this means consolidation moments distributed across the hour — at minute 15, at minute 35, at the end — beat a single review at the close.

**The strategies that feel like teaching are not always the strategies that produce learning.** Brown, Roediger, and McDaniel's *Make It Stick* ([2014, Belknap Press / Harvard University Press](https://www.hup.harvard.edu/books/9780674729018)) names this directly: fluent presentation, comprehensive coverage, and immediate feedback all *feel* like good teaching to the instructor and the student in the room. They correlate weakly — sometimes negatively — with retention. Retrieval practice, spaced retrieval, and what Bjork & Bjork (2011) call ["desirable difficulties"](https://bjorklab.psych.ucla.edu/wp-content/uploads/sites/13/2016/04/EBjork_RBjork_2011.pdf) feel harder for both parties. They produce more learning. A faculty member optimizing for the experience of the room is sometimes optimizing against the learning of the students in it.

Backward design is the structural move that lets the lecture serve learning rather than the experience of coverage. The deck — the artifact — is the visible expression of which one the instructor chose.

## 4. Bad example

The bad example is not a single slide. It is a deck structure. This matters; the failure is at the deck level, and showing it as a slide would miss the point.

Here is the structure of a 60-slide capital-structure deck as Gamma produces it from the textbook chapter outline.

```
DECK: Capital Structure (60 slides, 50-minute lecture)
================================================================

Section 1: Introduction (slides 1–3)
  Slide 1: Title — "Capital Structure"
  Slide 2: Learning objectives (5 bullets, each a topic)
  Slide 3: "What we'll cover today" — the table of contents

Section 2: Modigliani-Miller, No Taxes (slides 4–13)
  Slide 4: M-M Proposition I (statement)
  Slide 5: M-M Proposition I (the assumptions)
  Slide 6: M-M Proposition I (the proof sketch)
  Slide 7: M-M Proposition II (statement)
  Slide 8: M-M Proposition II (the math)
  Slide 9: WACC under M-M (definition)
  Slide 10: WACC under M-M (the formula)
  Slide 11: Example — Acme Corp under M-M
  Slide 12: Why the assumptions fail in practice
  Slide 13: Summary of M-M no-tax

Section 3: Modigliani-Miller with Taxes (slides 14–23)
  ... (10 more slides, same pattern as Section 2)

Section 4: Financial Distress (slides 24–33)
  ... (10 more slides)

Section 5: Pecking Order Theory (slides 34–43)
  ... (10 more slides)

Section 6: Signaling and Agency Costs (slides 44–53)
  ... (10 more slides)

Section 7: Conclusion (slides 54–60)
  Slide 54: Summary of M-M
  Slide 55: Summary of distress costs
  Slide 56: Summary of pecking order
  Slide 57: Summary of signaling
  Slide 58: Summary of agency costs
  Slide 59: "Putting it all together" (5 bullets, recap)
  Slide 60: Q&A
```

The structure problems are everywhere once you know to look. There is no hook — the deck opens with a title slide and a table of contents, neither of which gives the student a reason to care. The "learning objectives" on slide 2 are topics in disguise; they begin with verbs like *understand* and *know about*, which are not action verbs and not testable. Each section is a textbook section transposed: definition, then proposition, then math, then example, then summary. The whole hour proceeds at a uniform pace — about one minute per slide — with no consolidation moments and no retrieval practice. The deck ends with a recap and a "Q&A" slide that, in practice, means three questions from the same three students who always ask questions.

The deck has covered the territory. It has not built the student to anywhere in particular.

Now look at what happens to working memory during this deck. Slide 4 introduces M-M Proposition I. Slide 7 introduces Proposition II, which depends on understanding I. Slide 9 introduces WACC, which depends on II. By slide 11 (the worked example), the student is being asked to apply three concepts that have each had three slides of explanation and no opportunity to use. Their schema for Proposition I is fragile — they heard it three minutes ago and have not retrieved it since. The example pulls on a foundation the deck hasn't given them time to build. This is the **intrinsic load** failure mode at the deck level. The slides are individually fine. The sequence does not give the student room to consolidate before the next concept lands.

Add the **extraneous load** at the deck level: there is no signaling about *where in the arc* the student currently is. The slides do not say "this is part of the build" or "this is consolidation" — they all look like content slides. The student spends working memory tracking the position in the topic list, not building the concept. The slide-level analog is Mayer's Signaling Principle — at the slide level, tell the student what matters. The deck-level analog is: tell the student where they are in the arc, and what each part of the arc is for.

Finally, the **germane load**. There is none — or close to none. Germane load is the productive cognitive effort that builds schemas. It happens when the student is doing something with the material: solving a problem, predicting a result, retrieving a previously learned concept, explaining a step to a partner. The deck has zero such moments. Every minute is reception. The students leave able to recognize the terms because they have heard the terms repeatedly. They cannot produce explanations because they have never produced explanations during the lecture.

## 5. Good example

Same content. Restructured into three arcs of about 15 slides each, with two consolidation moments. The number of distinct *topics* the deck contains is roughly the same. The deck's *organization* is entirely different.

```
DECK: Capital Structure — Should Acme Corp Issue Debt? (45 slides, 50-min)
================================================================

ARC 1: The puzzle — and the irrelevance result (slides 1–13)
  Hook (slides 1–2)
    Slide 1: One claim, one company: "Acme Corp has $500M to raise.
             Debt or equity?"
    Slide 2: A poll question (clicker / hand-raise / Mentimeter):
             "How should the financing decision change the firm's value?"
             [The student commits to a prediction before the theory.]

  Build (slides 3–11)
    Slide 3: ASSERTION — "Under perfect markets, capital structure
             doesn't matter."
    Slides 4–6: M-M Proposition I, worked through Acme's balance
                sheet step by step. Each slide ends with the next
                cash flow line.
    Slides 7–9: M-M Proposition II — the rising cost of equity as
                leverage rises, derived visually from the no-arbitrage
                argument.
    Slide 10: WACC under M-M (the constant line on the WACC chart).
    Slide 11: Back to Acme — what M-M predicts about the $500M choice.

  Consolidate (slides 12–13)
    Slide 12: RETRIEVAL — "On your own paper, write the M-M Prop I
             argument in two sentences. (90 seconds.) Then turn to
             your neighbor and compare." [Forced retrieval. Every
             student attempts. The instructor walks the room.]
    Slide 13: REVEAL — the two-sentence version on the slide. Students
             check their own.

ARC 2: What's wrong with the answer — distress and taxes (slides 14–28)
  Hook (slide 14)
    Slide 14: "M-M says the choice doesn't matter. Why does every CFO
             still have an opinion?" [The next puzzle. The first
             arc's answer is being made unsatisfying on purpose.]

  Build (slides 15–25)
    Slides 15–18: Taxes break M-M. The interest tax shield, derived
                  on Acme's income statement. PV of tax shield as a
                  formula and a number for Acme.
    Slides 19–22: Financial distress costs. Direct (legal, fees) and
                  indirect (lost customers, fire-sale asset values).
                  Visualized as a function rising with leverage.
    Slides 23–25: The trade-off — tax shield benefit minus expected
                  distress cost. The optimal-leverage curve, with
                  Acme's marker.

  Consolidate (slides 26–28)
    Slide 26: WORKED EXAMPLE — Apply the trade-off to Acme. What's
             the optimal debt ratio given Acme's tax rate and
             distress probability? Instructor walks the calculation.
    Slide 27: RETRIEVAL — "A second firm, BravoCo, has high cash flow
             volatility. Same $500M decision. Does the optimal debt
             ratio go up or down — and by what mechanism?" [Students
             work in pairs for 2 minutes. Then we hear answers.]
    Slide 28: REVEAL — the answer and the reasoning trace.

ARC 3: When the trade-off is not enough — information and incentives
       (slides 29–45)
  Hook (slide 29)
    Slide 29: "Two firms with identical balance sheets choose
             different financing. The market reacts differently.
             What's the trade-off theory missing?"

  Build (slides 30–40)
    Pecking order (slides 30–33): information asymmetry, the equity-
                 issuance signal, why firms prefer internal funds.
    Signaling (slides 34–36): debt as commitment, why mature firms
              signal stability with debt.
    Agency costs (slides 37–40): debt as discipline (Jensen's free
                 cash flow), equity as flexibility, the trade-off
                 within the trade-off.

  Consolidate (slides 41–45)
    Slide 41: SYNTHESIS — Return to Acme. Apply all three layers
             (trade-off + pecking order + agency). What's your
             defended answer now?
    Slide 42: Class poll, second time. [Compare to slide 2.]
    Slide 43: One worked answer, defended on the slide.
    Slide 44: TRANSFER PROMPT — "BravoCo (volatile cash flows) and
             CharlieCorp (mature, low growth). One should issue debt,
             one should issue equity. Which is which, and defend with
             at least two of the three theories."
    Slide 45: Close. [The transfer prompt is the take-home; the
             reveal happens at the start of the next lecture.
             Distributed retrieval.]
```

Look at what the structure does. Each arc opens with a problem — Acme's $500M financing decision — that creates the *need to know*. The build introduces concepts in dependency order: M-M before trade-off, trade-off before information layers, because each later layer is a correction to the earlier one rather than a parallel topic. Each arc closes with retrieval practice or a worked example that requires the student to *use* the concept before the next arc begins. The deck ends with a transfer prompt the student has to think through between lectures, which produces both spacing and forced retrieval.

The students leave the lecture having done the thing the test will ask them to do. Not having heard about it. Done it.

Two structural facts worth noticing. First, the topic count is *not* dramatically smaller. The bad version covered M-M (with and without tax), distress, pecking order, signaling, agency. The good version covers the same concepts. What changed is the order, the integration, and the consolidation moments. Coverage is not the variable. Architecture is. Second, the bad version had 60 slides and the good version has 45. Slide count went down. This is what subtraction looks like at the deck level — and it almost always feels uncomfortable, because the instructor's gut sense of "thorough" reads slides-deleted as material-skipped. It is not. The material is the same; the build is what's different.

If the syllabus listing still feels like a constraint, the responsibility for "covering" the cut topics moves to the reading list. The textbook covers them. Problem sets reinforce them. The lecture builds the schema the student needs in order to use them. *Coverage* is what a course offers across all its components. *Teaching* is what the lecture, specifically, does.

## 6. The prompt

The Brutalist prompt for restructuring a coverage deck into an arc deck takes the topic list as input and forces the architectural questions before any slide regenerates.

```
Take this deck's topic list. Before producing any slides, do the
following analysis:

1. For each topic, write what the student should be able to DO with
   it after this lecture. Use action verbs (explain, apply, analyze,
   compare, evaluate, produce). If the only verb you can find is
   "know about" or "understand," reduce the topic's priority — it is
   probably a coverage topic, not a teaching topic.

2. Cluster the topics into 3–5 arcs. Each arc should:
   - Open with a problem or question that motivates the build
     (the hook)
   - Introduce concepts in dependency order — prerequisites first
     (the build)
   - Close with a retrieval-practice prompt or a worked example
     (the consolidate)

3. For each arc, identify the central problem. The same problem
   should reappear at the end as the consolidation moment. This
   gives the arc its closure.

4. Topics that do not fit any arc move to:
   - the reading list (the textbook covers them)
   - the problem set (the students apply them)
   - the discussion section (a TA can build them)
   They are not deleted from the course. They are deleted from
   the lecture deck.

5. Verify that every arc has at least one retrieval-practice slide.
   "Any questions?" is not retrieval. A clicker question, a written
   prompt, a paired discussion that every student attempts — that
   is retrieval.

6. Now generate the slides.
```

The prompt is doing instructional design work before any slide-design work. That is the move backward design demands. The slide generation is the last step, not the first, and the AI tool — Brutalist, Gamma, anything — is best used after the architecture is decided. Most AI slide tools default to forward design (topics-in, slides-out) and the instructor has to refuse that default explicitly.

## 7. The DESIGN.md change

Two variables in DESIGN.md operationalize the chapter:

```yaml
# DESIGN.md — deck-level architecture
deck_arc_count_target: 3-5
# A 50-minute lecture deck should have 3–5 narrative arcs, each
# containing hook / build / consolidate. Decks with more than 5
# arcs are usually coverage decks in disguise. Decks with fewer
# than 3 may be under-segmented for the working-memory
# constraints discussed below.

retrieval_practice_per_arc_min: 1
# Each arc must contain at least one retrieval-practice slide
# (clicker question, paired discussion prompt, written attempt,
# worked-example walk-through). "Q&A" and "any questions?" do
# not count — they are voluntary participation, not forced
# retrieval.

deck_opening_convention: hook_with_problem
# The first content slide of a deck is a problem, a question,
# or a phenomenon — not a title or a table of contents. The
# learning-objectives slide, if present, comes after the hook,
# not before. The hook is what creates the need for the
# objectives to exist.

segmentation_interval_target_minutes: 15-20
# Working-memory and attention research supports periodic
# consolidation moments at roughly 15–20-minute intervals
# during a live lecture. The exact figure is less settled than
# the principle (the "15 minutes" rule's empirical anchor is
# loose — see Hartley & Davies 1978, Bligh 2000, Guo et al.
# 2014; the 6-9 minute figure from edX video is for async, not
# live). Treat as a heuristic, not a hard rule. [verify]
```

The two non-negotiable variables are `deck_arc_count_target` and `retrieval_practice_per_arc_min`. These are the structural commitments. Everything else — segmentation interval, opening convention — is a default that the instructor can adjust for their specific content and audience.

## 8. The diagnostic questions to keep

For any deck, before slide one, and again at the dress rehearsal:

1. **What should the student be able to *do* after this lecture?** Action verb, single sentence. If you cannot answer this, the deck has no destination and will end up a coverage deck by default.
2. **Does the deck open with a problem or a table of contents?** Problem: the deck has a hook. Table of contents: the deck is signaling coverage from slide one.
3. **How many distinct arcs does the deck have, and what is each arc's central question?** If you cannot name the arcs, the deck is probably a topic list rather than a build.
4. **Where are the retrieval-practice moments?** Count them. If there are zero, the deck ends when the content ends and throws away the most powerful retention move available.
5. **Which topics could be moved to the reading list or the problem set without harming the lecture's arc?** Almost always more than the instructor's first answer.
6. **Could a student who attended this lecture answer the central question of the unit test?** Not "recognize the relevant terms." *Answer.* If the answer is "they would have heard about it," the deck taught coverage. If the answer is "they would have practiced producing the answer," the deck taught the thing.

These are the questions to ask before opening the slide tool, and the ones to ask again at the end of the build before walking into the lecture.

## 9. What two people might disagree about

The genuine disagreement is not whether backward design works. The component evidence is settled enough that the dispute about *that* is mostly defending old habits. The genuine disagreement is about the *constraint* — what an instructor is willing to give up in order to teach by arc rather than by coverage.

Two reasonable positions:

**The teaching-arc position:** A 50-minute lecture has room for 3–5 arcs. The reading list, problem sets, and discussion sections handle topics the lecture cannot teach without becoming a list. Coverage is a *course* property, not a *lecture* property. If the course covers a topic across its components — readings, lectures, problem sets, discussion, exams — coverage is satisfied. The lecture's job is the schema-build that the other components depend on but cannot do.

**The coverage-defense position:** Many students do not do the reading. The lecture is, in practice, the only place a substantial fraction of the class encounters the material. A deck that "teaches by arc" and leaves topics to the reading list is leaving those topics out for the half of the class that won't open the textbook. The teaching gain on the included topics may not offset the coverage loss on the excluded ones.

Both positions are defensible, and the empirical research does not yet directly settle which deck-design produces better aggregate course outcomes. The component evidence (retrieval practice, spacing, schema construction) supports arc decks for the topics they include. Whether the topics they exclude are worse off than they would have been in a coverage deck is harder to test. My current reading is that the arc deck wins on average, because the topics it teaches it teaches well and the topics it excludes are often topics that were not landing under coverage either — but I hold this with calibration, not certainty. A direct A/B comparison of coverage decks vs. arc decks on aggregate course performance would resolve the disagreement; I have not found one. [verify — possibly Freeman et al. 2014 PNAS active-learning meta-analysis touches this, but the active-learning bundle is broader than the deck-architecture question.]

The vocabulary for the disagreement is sharper than "I prefer comprehensive" vs. "I prefer focused." Two instructors disagreeing about a specific deck can converge by naming:

- The action the student should produce on the test (what does the deck need to build *to*?)
- The components of the course that handle the non-arc topics (which non-deck artifacts are doing the coverage work?)
- The empirical commitments each instructor is willing to make (which finding — backward design, retrieval, spacing — is each one most uncertain about, and which would they revise the deck design over?)

A disagreement at the level of those three questions is productive. A disagreement at the level of "but the syllabus says…" is not, because the syllabus is a coverage document and the lecture is not.

---

**What would change my mind:** A direct A/B experiment showing that decks designed under backward design (3–5 arcs, retrieval practice per arc, hook-build-consolidate) produce *equal or lower* aggregate course performance than coverage-organized decks of the same content, when matched for instructor, course, and student population. The component evidence is strong. The deck-architecture-level comparison is, as far as I have found, missing — and the strongest single piece of evidence that would revise my current reading.

**Still puzzling:** Why some instructors who are clearly excellent teachers use coverage-style decks and produce strong outcomes anyway. My best guess is that the instructor is doing the arc-building verbally, around a coverage-organized visual artifact — the deck is a list but the spoken lecture is a build. If true, the deck-as-artifact is misleading: it looks like a coverage deck and is functioning as a hook-build-consolidate lecture, with the architecture in the speaker rather than the slides. That would make the deck a worse study artifact (Chapter 9's concern) but a defensible live tool. I haven't tested this, and I'm not yet sure where the line is between "the instructor is carrying the architecture" and "the instructor is excellent in spite of the deck."
