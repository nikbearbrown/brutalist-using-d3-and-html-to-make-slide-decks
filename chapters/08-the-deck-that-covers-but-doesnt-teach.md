# Chapter 8 — The Deck That Covers but Doesn't Teach


## TL;DR

- What should the student be able to do after this lecture — and does the deck build them to that, or just list what you intended to mention?
- The chapter moves through The question that changes everything, Why coverage feels like teaching, What the architecture of the bad deck actually does, What a teaching deck looks like instead, and related ideas.
- Read it for the main argument, the vocabulary it introduces, and the practical judgment it asks you to develop.

*What should the student be able to do after this lecture — and does the deck build them to that, or just list what you intended to mention?*

---

Two weeks after the capital structure lecture, you give the unit test. The first question is the one you most want them to get right: *A mature firm with stable cash flows is considering raising $500 million. Walk through whether they should issue debt or equity, and defend your choice using the trade-off theory of capital structure.*

About a third of the class produces a coherent argument. About a third recognize the relevant concepts but cannot string them together. About a third write back definitions — what Modigliani-Miller is, what agency costs are, what the pecking order is — and stop. They could recognize every term you put in front of them. None of them could do the thing.

You gave the lecture. You covered sixty slides in fifty minutes. Modigliani-Miller no-tax, Modigliani-Miller with-tax, financial distress, pecking order theory, signaling, agency costs — every topic got ten clean slides. The deck did not miss anything. The students were attentive. You finished exactly at the bell.

What happened?

The deck covered the chapter. The deck did not teach the chapter. Those are different operations and they look identical from the front of the room.

---

## The question that changes everything

Here is the question that, asked before slide one, prevents most of what went wrong: *What should the student be able to do after this lecture?*

Not "know about." Not "have heard of." Not "be familiar with." Do. The difference between a coverage deck and a teaching deck almost always traces back to whether this question was asked and answered before the first slide was built.

If the answer is "students should be familiar with capital structure theories," the deck is going to be a list. There is nowhere for it to go except through the topics. If the answer is "students should be able to decide whether a firm with stable cash flows should take on debt, and defend the choice with the trade-off theory," the deck has a destination. The concept of destination changes every structural decision that follows.

Grant Wiggins and Jay McTighe named this the problem of "coverage" versus "uncoverage" in their framework for instructional design. Coverage, they argued, is the twin sin of instruction: either lots of activity without understanding, or lots of telling without doing. The corrective is structural and runs backwards from the outcome. Start from what the student should produce at the end. Work back to what evidence would show they can produce it. Only then design the instruction that builds toward it. The slide deck is the last step in that chain, not the first.

Most instructors invert the order. They start with the chapter — "what do I need to cover?" — generate slides around the table of contents, and only afterward, if at all, consider what students should be able to do. The deck is organized for the territory, not for the journey through it.

## Why coverage feels like teaching

The confusion between covering and teaching is not carelessness. It is structural, and it lives in a specific property of working memory.

When you explain something clearly and a student nods and says "I understand," both of you have received a signal that something happened. The student processed the explanation. They constructed a mental representation of the concept. They can, right now, recognize a correct definition if you present one. Recognition feels like learning because it is learning — a weak form of it.

The problem is that recognition and production are different cognitive skills, and they do not transfer to each other reliably. Jeffrey Karpicke and Henry Roediger demonstrated this in a 2008 experiment published in *Science*. Students either studied a passage four times or studied it once and recalled it from memory three times. One week later, the recall group remembered roughly sixty percent of the material; the restudy group remembered roughly forty percent. The more striking finding was this: students in the restudy condition felt more confident. They recognized the material fluently. They had learned less.

![The students who learned less were more sure they had learned. This is the mechanism behind the coverage trap.](images/08-the-deck-that-covers-but-doesnt-teach-fig-02.png)
*Figure 8.2 — Bar chart of the Karpicke-Roediger 2008 result *

What this means for a lecture deck is precise. Every minute of the lecture spent on explanation is a minute of restudy. The student is hearing, organizing, recognizing. They are not retrieving. And the retrieval moment — the forced attempt to produce the concept from memory — is the moment that actually does the learning. A deck that ends when the content ends has run fifty minutes of restudy and zero minutes of retrieval. The students leave able to recognize the terms. The exam asks them to produce an argument.

There is a companion finding from the spacing literature. Nicholas Cepeda and colleagues, in a 2006 meta-analysis covering 317 experiments and 839 effect sizes, found that practice distributed across time produces substantially better long-term retention than the same amount of practice concentrated in one session. For a lecture, this means that consolidation moments distributed through the hour — at minute fifteen, at minute thirty, at the close — beat a single review at the end. The students in the capital structure lecture got one review: slides fifty-four through fifty-nine, summarizing each section in turn. The review was a restudy exercise disguised as consolidation.

Peter Brown, Henry Roediger, and Mark McDaniel, synthesizing this literature in *Make It Stick*, put the knife in directly: the strategies that feel like good teaching to the instructor and the student in the room are often not the strategies that produce learning. Fluent explanation, comprehensive coverage, and immediate recognition all produce a feeling of success. They correlate weakly — sometimes negatively — with retention measured one week later. Retrieval practice, spaced retrieval, and what Robert and Elizabeth Bjork call "desirable difficulties" feel harder for everyone in the room. They produce more learning. An instructor optimizing for the experience of the lecture is sometimes optimizing against the learning of the students.

## What the architecture of the bad deck actually does

Return to the sixty-slide capital structure deck and watch what it does to a student's working memory minute by minute.

Slide four introduces Modigliani-Miller Proposition I. Slide seven introduces Proposition II, which depends on understanding Proposition I. Slide nine introduces WACC under M-M, which depends on Proposition II. By slide eleven — the worked example — the student is being asked to apply three concepts that have each had three slides of explanation and no opportunity to use. Their schema for Proposition I is fragile. They heard it eight minutes ago and have not retrieved it since. The example pulls on a foundation the deck has not given them time to build.

This is the intrinsic load failure at the deck level. The individual slides are fine. The sequence does not give the student room to consolidate before the next concept arrives.

Then there is the extraneous load at the deck level. The sixty slides have no signaling about where the student is in the arc. Every slide looks like a content slide. The student spends working memory tracking their position in the topic list rather than building the concept. The slide-level analog is Mayer's Signaling Principle — tell the student what matters. The deck-level analog is: tell the student where they are in the build, and what each part of the build is for.

And finally, the germane load. There is none. Germane load is the productive cognitive work that builds schemas — solving a problem, predicting a result, retrieving a previously learned concept, explaining a step to a partner. The deck has zero such moments. Fifty minutes of reception. The students leave able to recognize the terms because they heard the terms spoken. They cannot produce explanations because they have never produced explanations during the lecture.

## What a teaching deck looks like instead

The same content, restructured, looks like three arcs of roughly fifteen slides each, with consolidation moments between them.

The first arc opens with a hook: not a title slide, not a table of contents, but a problem. Acme Corp has five hundred million dollars to raise. Debt or equity? Before any theory is introduced, the student commits to a prediction — a hand-raise, a clicker vote, a written answer, anything that requires them to stake a position. The commitment is the cognitive move that makes the subsequent theory land. They have a prediction to compare it to.

Then the arc builds M-M in dependency order. Proposition I first, derived through Acme's balance sheet step by step. Proposition II from Proposition I. WACC from Proposition II. The worked example is not separated from the derivation — it runs alongside it, so the student is using each piece as it arrives. The arc closes with a retrieval moment. Not "any questions?" — a forced retrieval, every student: write the M-M Proposition I argument in two sentences, then compare with a neighbor. Ninety seconds. The instructor walks the room. Then the two-sentence version appears on the slide. The students check their own.

The second arc opens with the question that makes the first arc's answer unsatisfying: if M-M is right that capital structure doesn't matter, why does every CFO have an opinion? The new puzzle creates the need for the corrective. Taxes break M-M. The interest tax shield is derived on Acme's income statement, not stated as a formula. Financial distress costs are visualized as a function rising with leverage. The trade-off between them — the optimal-leverage curve — gives the student a tool for Acme's specific decision. The arc closes with a second retrieval: apply the trade-off to a different firm, one with volatile cash flows. Does the optimal debt ratio go up or down? By what mechanism? Students work in pairs. Then we hear answers.

The third arc asks what the trade-off theory is missing. Two firms with identical balance sheets choose different financing and the market reacts differently. Information asymmetry, the equity-issuance signal, the pecking order, agency costs — these are now corrections to a framework the student has been building for thirty minutes rather than new topics arriving in sequence. The arc closes with a synthesis: return to Acme, apply all three layers, produce a defended answer. Then a second clicker poll, the same question as slide two. The comparison shows the student what the lecture built.

The deck ends not with a summary but with a transfer prompt that goes home with the student: two different firms, one should issue debt and one should issue equity, defend with at least two of the three theories. The reveal comes at the start of the next lecture. Distributed retrieval. The learning continues between sessions because the architecture requires it to.

![Same content. Forty-five slides instead of sixty. The arc structure gives every concept a consolidation moment before the next one arrives.](images/08-the-deck-that-covers-but-doesnt-teach-fig-04.png)
*Figure 8.4 — Three-arc deck structure shown as a horizontal flow*

This is what backward design produces in a fifty-minute lecture. The topic count is not dramatically smaller. Modigliani-Miller, distress costs, pecking order, signaling, agency costs — the same list. What changed is the order, the integration, and the consolidation moments. The students leave having done the thing the test asks them to do. Not having heard about it. Done it.

## The structural move that coverage prevents

There is a moment in every coverage deck where the instructor silently makes a choice without realizing it. The choice is: *which is my job — to cover the terrain, or to build the student to a destination?*

Coverage says: my job is to move through the material. The deck is organized around the textbook because the textbook organizes the material correctly. If a student leaves with every topic checked, the lecture succeeded.

Teaching-to-destination says: my job is to produce a student who can do something they could not do when they walked in. The deck is organized around the build the student needs. Some topics may not survive the architecture — not because they are unimportant, but because they are not part of the build to this destination. They go to the reading list, the problem set, the discussion section. They are not deleted from the course. They are deleted from the lecture.

This is the change that feels like loss. An instructor who has organized the course around a textbook experiences topics-deleted-from-the-lecture as material-skipped. It is not. The course offers coverage across all its components. The lecture, specifically, does the schema-build that the other components depend on but cannot do. A reading assignment can introduce a concept. A problem set can require its application. The lecture can create the experience of *needing* to understand something before you can proceed — which is a different cognitive operation entirely.

The discomfort is real and worth naming. A forty-five-slide arc deck looks thinner than a sixty-slide coverage deck. The instructor's gut sense of "thorough" reads slides-deleted as rigor-reduced. This is the coverage instinct operating on the wrong variable. Rigor is not the number of topics. Rigor is whether the student can use what they were taught.

---

## What the coverage defense gets right

There is an honest position on the other side of this, and I want to give it its due.

Many students do not do the reading. The lecture is, in practice, the only place a substantial fraction of the class encounters the material. A deck organized around arcs and consolidation moments, which assigns non-arc topics to the reading list, is leaving those topics uncovered for the portion of the class that will not open the textbook.

This is a real concern. The arc deck wins on the topics it includes — it teaches them with retrieval, spacing, and productive struggle, which the research supports. Whether that gain offsets the coverage loss on excluded topics depends on two things the research does not yet directly settle: how much the uncovered topics were actually landing under the coverage deck, and whether the improved depth on arc-topics has downstream effects on related concepts in subsequent lectures.

My current reading is that the arc deck wins on average, because the topics it excludes were often not landing under coverage either — they were heard without being used, and hearing without using produces recognition that degrades within days. But I hold this with calibration. A direct comparison of coverage-organized versus arc-organized decks measuring aggregate course performance — not just the topics the arc deck covers, but the whole course — is the experiment I have not found. Until someone runs it cleanly, the evidence is strong at the component level and uncertain at the deck-architecture level.

The vocabulary for resolving this disagreement is sharper than preferences. Two instructors debating a specific deck can converge by naming: what the student must produce on the test; which course components handle the non-arc topics; and which empirical findings each instructor is most uncertain about. A disagreement at that level is productive. A disagreement at the level of "but the syllabus says we have to cover this" is not, because the syllabus is a coverage document and the lecture does not have to be.

## The question that reveals which kind of deck you have

Before you build a slide, before you open the tool, ask the question at the top of this chapter: what should the student be able to do after this lecture?

If you can answer it in one sentence with an action verb — explain, apply, analyze, compare, evaluate, produce — you have a destination. The deck can be built backward from it.

If the answer is "be familiar with the main theories of capital structure," you have a topic list. The deck will be a coverage deck by default because you have not given it anywhere to go.

The answer is not graded on ambition. A destination can be modest. "Students should be able to identify which of the three theories applies to a given firm's financing decision and state the mechanism" is a smaller claim than "students should be able to build a fully defended optimal capital structure for a real firm." Both are destinations. Both are testable. Both will produce a different deck than "students should know the main theories."

Most decks never ask the question. That is why most decks cover without teaching.

---

**What would change my mind:** A direct A/B experiment showing that coverage-organized decks produce equal or higher aggregate course performance than arc-organized decks of the same content, when matched for instructor, course, and student population. The component evidence — retrieval practice, spacing, schema construction — favors the arc deck for the topics it includes. Whether the topics it excludes are worse off than they would have been under coverage is the question the component evidence cannot answer. That experiment, done cleanly, is what would revise my current reading.

**Still puzzling:** Some instructors who are clearly excellent teachers use coverage-style decks and produce strong outcomes anyway. My best guess is that the instructor is doing the arc-building verbally — the deck is a list but the spoken lecture is a build, with the hooks, the questions, the consolidation moments delivered in the instructor's voice rather than on the slides. If that is right, the coverage deck is a worse study artifact but a defensible live tool in the hands of an expert who is supplying the architecture orally. The line between "the instructor is carrying the architecture" and "the instructor is excellent in spite of the deck" is one I have not been able to draw cleanly.

## LLM Exercises

**Exercise 1 — Destination test**
Take any deck you have built or are planning. Write down your current learning objectives in whatever form they are in. Paste them to an LLM with this prompt: *"Evaluate each of these learning objectives. For each one, answer: (a) does it contain an action verb that describes something a student could produce or perform, or is it a recognition/familiarity verb like 'understand' or 'know about'? (b) Could you write a test question that would tell you unambiguously whether the student had achieved it? Rewrite any that fail (b) as testable action-verb objectives."* Use the rewritten objectives as the destination before building a single slide.

**Exercise 2 — Arc identification**
Paste the slide titles (or a topic list) from an existing deck to an LLM with this prompt: *"Identify whether this deck has a narrative arc structure (hook → build → consolidate, repeated) or a coverage structure (topic → topic → topic). If it is coverage-structured, propose a reorganization into 3–5 arcs. For each arc, name: the hook question that opens it, the dependency order in which the concepts should be introduced, and the retrieval-practice prompt that closes it."* Compare the proposed structure to your original and identify which topics would move to other course components.

**Exercise 3 — Retrieval practice audit**
Paste your full deck structure to an LLM with this prompt: *"Count the number of retrieval-practice moments in this deck — slides that require every student to produce something (a written answer, a pair discussion, a clicker vote, a prediction). 'Any questions?' does not count. 'Here is a summary' does not count. Return the count and the slide numbers. Then calculate: what percentage of the lecture time is spent on retrieval vs. reception?"* If the retrieval percentage is below 20%, identify where in each arc a consolidation moment could be inserted.

**Exercise 4 — Coverage triage**
List all the topics your deck currently covers. Ask an LLM: *"I am designing a 50-minute lecture. My destination is: [your one-sentence action-verb objective]. Here are all the topics I plan to cover: [list]. For each topic, classify it as: (a) essential to the arc — the student cannot reach the destination without it; (b) supporting — it enriches the arc but the destination is reachable without it; or (c) parallel coverage — it is important to the course but not load-bearing for this lecture's destination. For category (c) topics, suggest which other course component (reading, problem set, discussion section) handles them."* Use the classification to cut or reassign category (c) items from the deck.

**Exercise 5 — Transfer prompt design**
At the end of building an arc deck, ask an LLM: *"My lecture's destination is: [objective]. My final consolidation moment is: [description]. Design a transfer prompt — a question the student takes home that requires them to apply the lecture's concepts to a new situation they have not seen during the lecture. The prompt should: (a) require at least two of the lecture's core concepts, (b) be answerable in 10–15 minutes of thought, and (c) have a reveal that could open the next lecture. The reveal should confirm a correct answer while adding one concept the next lecture will build on."* Use the transfer prompt as the deck's closing slide rather than a summary.
